Why I Work So Hard

It was 3 AM in the morning. I sat there, on my overwhelmingly comfortable sofa bed, watching an episode of The Office. It was the last day of break and I still had loads of assignments to finish, but I HAD to finish my favorite show. “I worked my butt off throughout school so far, I think I deserve a few more episodes,” I thought to myself, trying to justify my unwise decision. My mouse hovered over the “next episode” button, when I looked over to the next room to see my Dad still up.

I double checked the clock: 3:30 AM. He was hunched over a small laptop, and his eyes were struggling to stay open because he also stayed up the night before. Immense concern fell over me, as I remembered my Dad's increasing back issues and his upcoming business trip. I got up from my seat and approached him. “Aren't you tired?” I carefully asked in Korean. “I'm doing okay!” he replied with a smile on his face. No matter how difficult things got, no matter how fatigued and exhausted his body became, no matter how many business trips he was forced to go on and how many overtime hours he was called to endure, my Dad never complained about work.

Although his slowly deteriorating health says otherwise, my Dad constantly reassures me that he's fine and that work isn't too bad. Sometimes he even has the nerve to tell me that I work too hard, and that I should rest. But how could I? My Dad, with all the struggle and confusion he tolerated as a Korean immigrant, purposefully and selflessly gave up his comfort and desires so that my family and I can have the greatest, most convenient, lavish life possible. And he does it all without a single complaint, because he simply loves his family.

When I feel overwhelmed with work and stressed by the pressures of life around me, I am reminded by the image of my Dad, hunched over his tiny laptop, struggling to stay awake. He is the reason why I push myself in all that I do and the symbol of selfless love that I strive to emulate. My Dad, the kindest, most trustworthy man I know, taught me to value my Korean American identity and treasure the opportunities he has created for me. How could I give anything less than my 100% when my Dad is giving even more?

I smiled at my Dad, went back to my comfortable sofa bed and turned off Netflix. The Office can wait.
